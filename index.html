📘 Essential Grammar for Cybersecurity Engineers

1. Present Simple vs. Present Continuous

Present Simple – facts, routines, and permanent states.

"I configure firewalls every day."
"The firewall blocks malicious traffic."

Present Continuous – actions happening right now or temporary situations.

"I am configuring the VPN at the moment."
"We are testing the new IPS signatures this week."
💡 Tip: Use Simple for facts ("The system runs on FortiOS 7.2"), Continuous for ongoing tasks ("I am troubleshooting a connectivity issue").

2. Present Perfect (Past → Present)

Used for past experiences with present relevance, or actions that started in the past and continue.

"I have configured this policy before."
"We have been working on this project for three months."
"The administrator has already applied the patch."
💡 Tip: Use "has/have + past participle". Very common in status reports: "The issue has been resolved."

3. Past Simple vs. Past Perfect

Past Simple – completed actions in the past.

"I configured the interface yesterday."

Past Perfect – an action that happened before another past action.

"I had already saved the config before I rebooted the device."
💡 Tip: Use Past Perfect to show sequence in troubleshooting: "The log showed that the user had attempted to log in three times."

4. Conditionals (If / When / Unless)

Zero Conditional (General facts):

"If the traffic matches the policy, it is allowed."

First Conditional (Real future possibility):

"If we enable this rule, the system will block the attack."
"Unless we update the signatures, we won't detect the new malware."

Second Conditional (Hypothetical / unlikely):

"If I had more bandwidth, I would reduce the latency."

Third Conditional (Past hypothetical):

"If we had applied the patch earlier, the breach would not have happened."
💡 Tip: In IT documentation, First and Zero conditionals appear most frequently.

5. Passive Voice (Very common in tech)

Used when the action is more important than the doer, or the doer is unknown.

"The firewall is configured by the admin."
"The logs are reviewed daily."
"The patch was applied last night."
"The vulnerability has been patched."
💡 Tip: Be careful: Passive voice can sound impersonal. Use it when the actor is obvious ("The route was added.") or unknown ("The system was breached.").

6. Modal Verbs (Obligation, Possibility, Ability)

  • Must – strong obligation: "You must enable 2FA."
  • Must not – prohibition: "You must not expose the admin interface to the internet."
  • Should – recommendation: "You should back up the config before the upgrade."
  • Might / Could – possibility: "The issue might be related to DNS." / "It could be a routing problem."
  • Can / Could – ability or request: "I can troubleshoot this." / "Could you check the logs?"
  • May – formal permission or possibility: "The system may reboot after the update."
  • Need to – necessity: "We need to review the firewall rules."
💡 Tip: "Must" is stronger than "should". In incident reports, use "must" for critical actions.

7. Prepositions (Place, Time, Direction)

  • On – surface / device: "I work on the firewall." / "The error is on the dashboard."
  • In – inside / context: "The error is in the log file." / "In this case, we block the traffic."
  • To – direction: "I send traffic to the gateway." / "Navigate to the CLI."
  • For – purpose / duration: "I am responsible for security." / "The session is valid for 8 hours."
  • At – specific point: "Look at the packet capture." / "The meeting is at 3 PM."
  • With – using / accompanied by: "I configured it with the CLI."
  • From – origin: "The traffic originates from the internal network."

8. Articles – Common mistakes

A / An – first mention, non-specific.

"I configured a firewall policy." (first time mentioned)

The – specific, already known.

"I configured the firewall policy we discussed." (both know which one)

Zero article – plural / general concepts.

"Firewalls are essential for network security." (general)
💡 Tip: Use "the" when there is only one (e.g., the internet, the administrator).

9. Relative Clauses (who, which, that, whose)

Used to add extra information about a person, object, or situation.

"The engineer who configured the network is on leave." (person)
"The policy that blocks port 443 caused the issue." (object/defining)
"The IPS signature, which was updated yesterday, detected the attack." (non-defining – with commas)
"The device whose IP address is 10.0.0.1 is offline." (possession)
💡 Tip: In technical writing, "that" is preferred for essential information (no commas). Use "which" with commas for extra, non-essential info.

10. Future Forms (Will / Going to / Present Continuous)

Will – spontaneous decisions, predictions, promises.

"The system will reboot automatically after the update." (prediction)
"I will check the logs right now." (spontaneous decision)

Going to – plans or intentions.

"We are going to deploy the patch next week." (plan)

Present Continuous – fixed arrangements.

"The meeting is starting at 10 AM." (schedule)
💡 Tip: In incident response, use "will" for immediate actions ("I will investigate") and "going to" for planned changes.

11. Phrasal Verbs – Essential

  • Set up – configure: "I need to set up the VPN."
  • Log in / Log on – authenticate: "Please log in to the FortiGate."
  • Shut down – power off: "We shut down the server for maintenance."
  • Roll back – revert to a previous state: "We rolled back the firmware update."
  • Wipe out – delete completely: "The attack wiped out the database."
  • Carry out – perform: "We carry out security assessments quarterly."
  • Break down – stop working: "The service broke down after the upgrade."
  • Filter out – remove: "We filter out malicious payloads."
💡 Tip: Phrasal verbs are very common in spoken IT English. Use them to sound more natural.

12. Asking Questions (Politely & Directly)

Direct questions (informal / urgent):

"How do you configure NAT?"
"Why is the interface down?"

Indirect / Polite questions (formal / to managers):

"Could you explain how to configure the SD-WAN?"
"I was wondering if you could check the logs."
"Would you mind reviewing the policy?"

Question tags (checking understanding):

"We need to apply the patch, don't we?"
💡 Tip: In emails and meetings with stakeholders, use indirect questions to sound more professional.

13. Adverbs of Frequency & Degree

Frequency – how often:

"I always verify the logs before applying changes." (100%)
"We usually perform updates on Sundays." (80%)
"The system sometimes crashes during peak hours." (50%)
"They rarely change the default settings." (20%)
"He never connects without the VPN." (0%)

Degree – intensity:

"It is very important to enable 2FA."
"The firewall is extremely sensitive to this rule."
"This version is slightly faster than the previous one."
💡 Tip: Place frequency adverbs before the main verb ("I always check") but after "to be" ("The system is usually stable").

14. Comparative & Superlative

Comparative – comparing two things:

"AES-256 is more secure than DES."
"This interface is faster than the old one."
"The new firmware is less stable than the previous version."

Superlative – the highest degree (three or more):

"This is the most efficient method we have."
"FortiGate is the best solution for our network."
💡 Tip: For short adjectives (fast, slow, big), add "-er" and "-est" (faster, fastest). For longer ones (secure, efficient), use "more" and "most".

15. Countable vs. Uncountable Nouns – IT warning

Countable – can be counted (plural possible):

"firewalls", "policys", "logs", "devices"
"The logs are stored in the database." (countable plural)

Uncountable – no plural, use singular verb:

"Data is stored on the server." (not "datas")
"Traffic is flowing through the interface."
"Equipment needs to be replaced." (no plural)
"Software is installed on the system."
"Information is confidential."
💡 Tip: Common mistakes: "Datas" (❌) → "Data" (✅), "Informations" (❌) → "Information" (✅). Use "a piece of information" or "a log entry" to count them.

⚠️ Quick Fix – Common Mistakes

  • ❌ Explain me → ✅ Explain to me / Explain how (never "explain me").
  • ❌ I am agree → ✅ I agree (no "am").
  • ❌ It depends of → ✅ It depends on.
  • ❌ We are discussing about → ✅ We are discussing (no "about").
  • ❌ We need to discuss about the policy → ✅ We need to discuss the policy.
  • ❌ The system is more better → ✅ better or more reliable (avoid double comparatives).
